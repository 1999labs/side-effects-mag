# Calling It AI

In May 2026, an anonymous account posted a Claude Monet to X with the caption "AI Monet, what's missing?" Thousands of replies followed. Painters dissected the brushwork. Critics flagged a flatness in the color field. Marc Andreessen tweeted about it roughly thirty times. Some explained, calmly and at length, why the image lacked the soul of a real Monet.

The image was a real Monet. *Water Lilies*, circa 1915.

The artist, [SHL0MS](https://www.sleek-mag.com/article/how-shl0ms-turned-a-monet-water-lilies-painting-into-viral-performance-art-by-calling-it-ai/), ran the audit. The audit failed.

What the performance revealed is bigger than a viral prank. Since photography arrived in 1840, the price of "made by hand" was an unstated baseline. Painters were assumed to be painting. Drawings came from drawers. For the first time in almost two centuries, the audience cannot reliably tell what made an image. And the response to that uncertainty is not curiosity. It is suspicion, then performance, then status.

## What people are actually buying

The first downstream effect is in commission rates. Working illustrators in 2026 are no longer paid for quality or speed. They are paid for proof of process. Public Patreon tiers now routinely advertise "watch the canvas," "time lapse video included," "signed working file with full layer history." The artist is not selling the image. The image is cheap. The artist is selling the audit trail.

The second downstream effect is in what gets shared. The image that gets reposted is no longer the one that is best. It is the one whose attribution is most defensible. A flawless illustration with no process video gets accused of being AI in the comments. A rougher illustration with a thirty second time lapse goes viral. The platform algorithms have not changed. The audience has changed what it is grading for.

The third downstream effect is at the bottom of the funnel. Junior illustrators report that the easiest gigs to win are not the ones that demand the best portfolio. They are the ones where the client wants to be in the room while you draw. Streaming has replaced sketching in some commission flows entirely. The work product is the same. The product is now the witness.

## The pattern is older than this

These are status signals. The same kind that "hand stitched" became to clothing after the sewing machine, or "wood fired" became to pizza after gas ovens. The default becomes artificial, so the deliberate becomes luxurious. What is new is the speed. With pizza it took a century. With painting it took eighteen months.

## What is new is older than that

There is a tradition for what SHL0MS did, and it is not in the gallery. In the late 1990s, a loose group of artists used the browser itself as their canvas. Olia Lialina. Vuk Ćosić. JODI. Heath Bunting. They called it net.art. The work was rarely an image you looked at. It was a small program the audience ran by visiting a URL. Sometimes the program lied to you about what it was. Sometimes it crashed your browser. The point was that the medium of the work was the medium of its delivery, and the audience's interaction was where the work happened.

Net.art lost institutional momentum once the web became professional. The questions it asked never did. SHL0MS posting a Monet on X and asking the audience to grade it is a net.art piece. The medium is the timeline. The work is the response. The image is the bait.

The SHL0MS performance matters because it exposes the engine the platforms are now running for free. The audience was not detecting AI. The audience was performing the act of detecting AI, in front of other audience members, for credibility. The image was not being graded. The grader was being graded. When someone calls a picture AI on the internet now, they are not making a claim about the picture. They are auditioning for membership in the group of people who can supposedly tell.

## The cost

The cultural side effect is that visual literacy and visual taste have started to come apart. The audience that loudly rejects an image as soulless is often less able to identify a real Monet than a casual viewer with no stake in the question. Stake corrupts perception. The discourse selects for confident misreaders.

This is a transient condition. It holds only while AI image quality is improving fast enough that the line keeps moving. By 2029, the floor will stabilize and the audience will stop trying to read the line, the way it stopped trying to detect Photoshop. "Is this AI" as a comment will date like "shopped or didn't happen." At that point the hand premium collapses to roughly ten percent of what it is today. Not the default. A collector's surcharge.

The pieces being made today will outlast the audit. The audits will not.
